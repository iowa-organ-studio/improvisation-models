Buxtehude Chorale Prelude — Getting Started

What am I looking at?

This trainer presents a phrase from a Buxtehude Chorale Prelude in three levels – the Chorale, the Harmonic Reduction and the Foreground.

How should I practice?

Start by just playing the phrases in the Foreground in different keys. You need to get a feel for how he constructed them, and what kind of tempo feels right to you. Try to play in as many keys as you’re comfortable with.

Then try hiding the Foreground and realize the Harmonic Reduction in four parts (i.e., play the figured bass). It’s of crucial importance that you use the same tempo that you played the Foreground.

Why? Once you get good at playing the reduction at performance tempo, your brain will start thinking, “This is boring! It needs more spice!” That’s the precise moment when you’re truly ready to embellish it. Start adding passing tones in different voices (don’t just play them in the soprano).

When you get stuck, open up the Foreground and see what Buxtehude did. If you like what he did, then close the Foreground and try doing that thing in as many places as possible.

After you start developing good embellishment strategies, then you can work on making up your own harmonic background. Open only the Chorale and try making your own "Harmonic Reduction." You'll see right away that even though Buxtehude's harmony seems simple, it's really ingenious. After you try a few times on a Chorale phrase, open up the Harmonic Reduction. Take note of how many figures he uses per Cantus Firmus note. Also notice how freely he alters the Chorale to suit his harmonic needs. Finally, when you discover a cool chord progression, go to the Random Hymn Tune generator and try to use that progression in every single hymn -- that will really cement it in your ear.

Finally, don’t worry if yours don’t sound as good as Buxtehude right away. We’re trying to learn how to “think on our feet,” so it’s not going to sound as good as a written-down piece. Don’t force too many embellishments into your improvisation. Let them bubble up naturally from your ears.

Historic vs Modern

The Historic toggle uses old conventions for figures. You’ll see slashed 6s and 4s, which sometimes mean “sharp” and sometimes mean “natural.” You’ll see flat-5, which many times doesn’t refer to an actual flatted note.

These conventions make a lot of sense when you’re within a few sharps/flats, but once you venture out to C♯ major and A♭ minor, they make less sense.

Modern shows exactly what accidental is required from the figure. You might think that this is obviously better, but it causes its own problems.

Transposing

Use the + and − buttons to move the phrase into different keys. The figured bass symbols update automatically.

The ♯/♭ button toggles between enharmonic equivalents — for example between F♯ major and G♭ major.

Filters

Use the key filter buttons to generate phrases only in keys with a specific number of sharps or flats.

Use the Major/Minor buttons to generate only phrases from Chorale Preludes in major or minor. Of course, there are sometimes phrases in Major chorales that are temporarily in a minor key, and vice versa.

The ♯/♭ button toggles between enharmonic equivalents — for example between F♯ major and G♭ major.
